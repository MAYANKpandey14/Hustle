# The Impact of Global Conflicts on the Crude Oil Supply Chain (2024–2026)

## Executive Summary

The period between 2024 and 2026 has witnessed the most significant structural realignment of the global crude oil supply chain since the 1970s. Driven by the protracted Russia-Ukraine war, chronic instability in the Red Sea, and a major supply shock in the Middle East in early 2026, the industry has shifted from "just-in-time" efficiency to a "just-in-case" resilience model. This report details the "Great Re-routing," the emergence of a bifurcated market, and the systemic impacts of maritime chokepoint closures on global energy security.

***

## 1. The Russia-Ukraine War: A Permanent Structural Realignment

By 2026, the initial shock of the 2022 invasion has solidified into a permanent bifurcation of the global oil market. The "Great Re-routing" of Russian oil has effectively split the world into two distinct trading blocs.

### The Pivot to Asia

Russia has successfully redirected approximately **4–5 million barrels per day (mb/d)** of crude and refined products away from the G7 and EU. China and India now account for over 80% of Russian seaborne crude exports. This shift is not merely logistical but financial, with an increasing share of these trades settled in **Yuan and Rubles**, directly challenging the long-standing "petrodollar" dominance.

### The "Shadow Fleet" Phenomenon

To bypass G7 price caps and Western insurance restrictions, Russia and its partners have institutionalized a "shadow fleet" of over 600 aging tankers. By early 2026, nearly **48% of Russian seaborne oil** was transported via these non-sanctioned vessels, creating a parallel logistics infrastructure that operates outside the transparency of traditional maritime oversight.

***

## 2. The 2026 Middle East Supply Shock: The Largest Disruption in History

While the 2024–2025 period was characterized by managed volatility, February 2026 saw a systemic collapse of the Middle Eastern supply chain following military escalations involving Iran, Israel, and the United States.

### The De Facto Closure of the Strait of Hormuz

In March 2026, the Strait of Hormuz—the world’s most critical energy artery—was effectively closed to commercial traffic. This blocked approximately **20 mb/d** of oil and petroleum products, representing nearly **25% of global seaborne trade**.

- **Production Shut-ins:** Gulf producers (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait) were forced to cut production by over **10 mb/d** as storage facilities reached capacity and export routes were severed.
- **Infrastructure Attrition:** Drone and missile strikes damaged over 40 critical energy assets across the region, including major refineries in Bahrain and Abu Dhabi.

### The Fragmenting of OPEC+

On **May 1, 2026**, the United Arab Emirates (UAE) officially exited OPEC, seeking to maximize its own production and leverage its "chokepoint-free" export routes. This move signaled the functional end of the OPEC+ alliance’s ability to stabilize global prices through coordinated quotas, leaving the market in a state of hyper-volatility.

***

## 3. Maritime Logistics and the "Oil on Water" Crisis

The conflicts in the Red Sea (2024–2025) and the Persian Gulf (2026) have transformed maritime logistics into a high-cost, high-risk venture.

### Rerouting and the Cape of Good Hope

The de facto abandonment of the Suez Canal/Red Sea route by major carriers like Maersk and Hapag-Lloyd forced ships to travel around the Cape of Good Hope. This added **3,500 nautical miles** and **12–15 days** to the journey from the Middle East to Europe.

- **Bunker Demand:** The longer routes and increased vessel speeds required to maintain schedules added **150,000 bpd** to global bunker fuel demand.
- **Inventory in Transit:** By early 2025, the IEA reported "oil on water" (inventory stuck in transit) reached a record **1.9 billion barrels**, creating a "hidden" supply deficit that amplified price spikes when land-based inventories ran low.

***

## 4. Economic Consequences: The Global Transmission of Oil Shocks

The disruptions in the oil supply chain have acted as a "force multiplier" for global economic instability.

### Imported Inflation and the Middle Class

The surge in Brent prices—peaking at **$120/bbl in March 2026**—cascaded through the global economy.

- **Logistics Costs:** Fuel surcharges now account for up to **20% of the landed price** of consumer goods.
- **Agricultural Crisis:** The closure of Hormuz blocked 30% of global fertilizer exports, causing a **31% price spike** in early 2026. This has directly threatened food security in the Global South and significantly increased the monthly grocery bill for the middle class in the Global North.

### Strategic Reserves and the "Just-in-Case" Model

In response to the 2026 shock, nations have aggressively expanded their Strategic Petroleum Reserves (SPR). China reached nearly **1 billion barrels** in inventory by 2026, while the IEA coordinated the largest emergency release in history (400 million barrels) to prevent a total economic collapse in Q1 2026.

## 5. Regional Vulnerabilities

| Region     | Impact of 2026 Supply Shock                                 | Strategy                                                             |
| ---------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Europe** | Severe feedstock gaps; high energy-driven inflation.        | Accelerated US/Norway sourcing; energy-saving mandates.              |
| **India**  | Caught between US sanctions and the need for Russian crude. | Aggressive diversification; increased SPR filling.                   |
| **China**  | Massive reliance on Hormuz (~50% of imports).               | Deepening Yuan-based trade with Russia/Iran; massive coal buffer.    |
| **USA**    | Record production (13.6 mb/d) provides a buffer.            | Exporting at record levels to Europe; managing domestic price anger. |

***

## 6. Conclusion and 2027 Outlook

The global crude oil supply chain of 2026 is unrecognizable from the one that existed in 2021. The "efficiency first" era has been replaced by a **"Geopolitics First"** era. While the Strait of Hormuz is expected to gradually reopen by late 2026, the structural damage to regional infrastructure and the permanent realignment of Russian trade routes mean that energy prices will carry a "permanent war premium." The world is now defined by a bifurcated energy map, where security of the route is as important as the quantity of the crude.
