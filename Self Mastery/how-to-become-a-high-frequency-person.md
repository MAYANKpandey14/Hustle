# *How to Become a High Frequency Person*

## Source Information

- **Original link:** [YouTube Video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SbtAoxR5SuM)
- **Title:** *how to become a high frequency person.*
- **Creator:** Jack Hopkins
- **Platform:** YouTube
- **Published date:** January 12, 2026
- **Duration:** 22 minutes

## Executive Summary

- The video argues that people often chase external outcomes like money, status, relationships, or success because they believe those things will finally make them feel peace, love, joy, or acceptance.
- Jack Hopkins says this order is backwards: first change your inner state, then external results become easier to receive.
- He frames “high frequency” as living more often in emotions like peace, joy, love, acceptance, courage, and willingness rather than fear, guilt, shame, grief, or anger.
- A central principle in the video is: do not **need** the thing you want; once you are no longer emotionally dependent on it, you stop chasing and become more aligned.
- He connects self-love to self-trust, arguing that people become harsh toward themselves because they repeatedly break promises to themselves.
- His practical solution is self-mastery through small kept promises, such as committing to a few simple actions each day and following through consistently.
- The video also teaches reframing adversity as neutral or instructive rather than inherently bad.
- Another major idea is “extreme ownership,” meaning taking responsibility for your response to everything in your life instead of blaming others.
- He promotes abundance by giving what you want to receive, such as money, love, joy, or peace, even in small amounts.
- The final message is that inner fulfillment must come first, because external success without inner change still leaves a person empty.

## Detailed Breakdown

### 1. The Core Thesis

The video opens by contrasting two models of life. In the first model, people believe an external event or possession, such as money, a business, a car, or a relationship, will produce the emotional state they want. In the second model, which Hopkins endorses, a person first cultivates the emotional state they desire and then becomes more able to welcome the matching reality into their life.

He repeatedly returns to the idea that people do not actually want the object itself as much as the feeling they expect it to create. A car may symbolize accomplishment, a partner may symbolize love, and money may symbolize security or freedom, but the emotional need underneath is the real driver.

His argument is that chasing outcomes from a place of lack strengthens low emotional states like desire, fear, guilt, or shame. By contrast, learning to feel whole in the present reduces emotional desperation and changes how one acts.

### 2. “Do Not Need What You Need”

One of the clearest principles in the video is the idea that needing something too badly pushes it away. Hopkins says that chasing money, women, jobs, or opportunities from a needy state creates the wrong “frequency” and often makes the person act in ways that are anxious, desperate, or unattractive.

He uses simple examples: over-texting someone you want, begging for a job, or obsessing over an outcome. In his framework, this state communicates lack rather than readiness, and that lack disrupts your ability to receive what you want.

Educationally, this can be read as a lesson in emotional non-attachment. Whether or not one accepts the “frequency” language literally, the behavioral insight is recognizable: people often perform worse when they are overdependent on a result.

### 3. His Personal Story

Hopkins explains that nearly four years earlier he moved to Thailand after quitting his 9-to-5 job and saving some money, hoping to become a content creator. Once there, he found that he was not enjoying the freedom he had once wanted because he became fixated on immediate success, money, and recognition.

He says things got so difficult that he ran out of money and took out his last $5,000 on a credit card. At that point, instead of continuing to operate from stress and fear, he decided to be grateful for what he already had and to act like the successful version of himself in the present.

According to his story, within a month of this shift, some of his videos started doing well on YouTube, he launched his first online program, and he made $10,000 in a single month. He presents this as the moment that convinced him his internal state was affecting his external reality.

### 4. Self-Love Through Self-Trust

A major section of the video explains why many people are unkind to themselves. Hopkins argues that people do not trust themselves because they have repeatedly failed to do what they said they would do, such as waking up on time, avoiding a bad habit, or following through on a goal.

In his framing, this repeated self-betrayal creates an internal relationship of disappointment and even abuse. The person then looks in the mirror and no longer believes their own intentions, which weakens confidence and makes self-love feel impossible.

His proposed fix is not abstract affirmations alone but rebuilding trust through action. He advises making only a few small promises each day, keeping them consistently, and gradually increasing the difficulty over time.

### 5. Self-Mastery as Practice

He describes self-mastery as the practical path to becoming a “high frequency” person. The instruction is to stop making casual promises and instead be extremely careful with your word, especially with yourself.

His specific suggestion is to choose around three promises per day that are simple and realistic, such as waking up at a set time, going to the gym, or eating a planned meal. He says that within a week, this can begin to change confidence, and over a longer period it can transform identity.

This section is one of the most actionable parts of the video because it translates a broad spiritual claim into habit formation. In plain terms, he is saying that identity is strengthened when commitments become trustworthy.

### 6. Adversity and Interpretation

Another major theme is adversity. Hopkins says that events are not inherently good or bad; instead, people assign emotional weight to events through their mindset and “frequency.”

He describes adversity as a kind of test: when things go wrong, do you collapse back into fear, grief, and anger, or do you remain in peace, joy, and love? He says the goal is to smile in the face of adversity because hardship can become a lesson or catalyst rather than a defeat.

He supports this by saying that many of the best things in his life came from painful experiences, including dissatisfaction in his 9-to-5 job and losses that helped shape him.

### 7. Extreme Ownership

The video then shifts into responsibility. Hopkins argues that blaming other people for your life drains your power because you cannot control them, while taking ownership returns control to your own mind and behavior.

He says that anger often comes from focusing on what is outside your control. Acceptance and ownership, by contrast, allow a person to move toward the life they want because they stop giving emotional power away.

This part of the video closely aligns with more mainstream self-development ideas. Even without accepting every metaphysical claim he makes, the practical lesson is clear: responsibility can be psychologically empowering when it focuses attention on action rather than blame.

### 8. Abundance and Giving

In the final third of the transcript, Hopkins introduces abundance as a way to expand the reality you want. His principle is that if you want money, love, peace, or joy, you should become a source of those things first.

He gives examples from Thailand, saying that even when he was broke he tipped heavily and gave money away because he wanted to embody abundance rather than scarcity. He also extends the same logic to love and joy, suggesting that people who give love and create peace around others are more likely to receive those qualities in return.

He shares one story in which he gave a street musician around $20 to $50 and then shortly afterward received three sales for a program he was selling. He presents this as evidence that giving from abundance is often returned in amplified form.

## Key Insights

- The most important shift in the video is from outcome-dependence to state-dependence: do not wait for life to make you feel whole; practice feeling grounded before the outcome arrives.
- The strongest practical idea is that self-respect grows from kept promises, not just from positive thinking.
- The language of “frequency” is spiritual and metaphorical, but much of the advice can also be translated into psychology: emotional regulation, habit consistency, non-attachment, and responsibility.
- The video treats desire itself as risky when it becomes emotional dependence, not when it remains a healthy direction.
- A useful way to interpret the message is: act like the kind of person who could responsibly hold the thing you want before you have it.

## Actionable Learnings

### A 7-Day Practice Based on the Video

1. Choose three promises per day that are small and measurable, for example: wake at a fixed time, complete one workout, and avoid one known bad habit.
2. Do not add extra promises casually; the point is to rebuild trust by completing what you commit to.
3. Each evening, review whether you kept your word and note how your self-talk changes when you do.
4. When something goes wrong, pause before labeling it as bad and ask what lesson, adjustment, or training opportunity it contains.
5. Give one small form of abundance daily, such as kindness, encouragement, attention, gratitude, or a modest financial gesture if appropriate.

### Practical Interpretation

For a more grounded reading, the video is less about mystical attraction and more about alignment between identity, behavior, and emotional state. If your habits, inner dialogue, and daily conduct contradict your goals, your confidence will fracture; if they align, your behavior becomes steadier and more convincing.
